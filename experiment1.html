🔬 Detecting Iron Impurity in Ga-68 Eluate using DTPA

During routine radiolabelling with Ga-68, an unusual observation was made. After elution of the generator and subsequent labelling, the eluate stored in a glass vial turned greenish after approximately 48 hours. This raised concerns about possible metal impurities, especially iron.

It was suspected that the impurity may have been introduced via the needle or cannula used during elution. To confirm this, hydrogen peroxide and DTPA were added. The solution turned bright yellow immediately, indicating formation of an iron complex.

DTPA is an acyclic pentadentate chelating ligand that forms stable complexes with metal ions at room temperature. It binds Fe³⁺ strongly, and the resulting [Fe(DTPA)]²⁻ complex is yellow due to Fe³⁺’s electronic transitions. This confirmed the presence of oxidized iron (Fe³⁺) in the eluate — likely from Fe²⁺ oxidation.

This matters in Ga-68 radiolabelling, where metal ion impurities can reduce chelator efficiency. Chelators like:

  • 🔸 DOTA – used in DOTA-TATE, DOTA-TOC
  • 🔸 HBED-CC – used in Ga-68 PSMA-11
are sensitive to competing metal ions. Fe³⁺ can strongly bind HBED-CC, reducing Ga³⁺ incorporation.

The stability constant for HBED-CC with Fe³⁺ is log K ≈ 36.74, nearly as high as for Ga³⁺ (log K ≈ 37.73).
Reference: Wadas et al., J Nucl Med Biol (2010) — cited by Zhernosekov et al., J Nucl Med 2007; 48(10):1741–1748. Read Full Paper

This observation shows that even minor metal contamination — like from a cannula — can affect radiolabelling efficiency and reproducibility in the nuclear medicine lab.
