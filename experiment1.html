🔬 Detecting Iron Impurity in Ga-68 Eluate using DTPA

During routine radiolabelling with Ga-68, an unusual observation was made. After elution of the generator and subsequent labelling, the eluate stored in a glass vial turned **greenish** after approximately **48 hours**. This raised concerns about possible metal impurities, especially **iron**.

It was suspected that the impurity may have been introduced via the **needle or cannula** used during elution. To confirm this, **hydrogen peroxide** and **DTPA** (diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid) were added to the same vial. The solution turned **bright yellow immediately**, indicating a chemical reaction — likely the formation of an **iron–DTPA complex**.

DTPA is an **acyclic pentadentate chelating ligand** that quickly forms stable complexes with metal ions at room temperature. It binds Fe³⁺ very strongly, and the resulting **[Fe(DTPA)]²⁻** complex exhibits a characteristic **yellow color** due to Fe³⁺’s electronic transitions. This confirmed the presence of **oxidized iron (Fe³⁺)** in the eluate — probably converted from Fe²⁺ via atmospheric oxygen or peroxide.

This observation is crucial in the context of **Ga-68 radiolabelling**. Competing metal ions like Fe³⁺ can interfere with chelator-based binding, particularly with sensitive systems like:

  • 🔸 **DOTA** – used for peptides like DOTA-TATE or DOTA-TOC
  • 🔸 **HBED-CC** – used in Ga-68 PSMA-11
Both chelators can potentially form side complexes with Fe³⁺, reducing radiochemical yield or affecting biodistribution.

The **stability constant** (log K) for HBED-CC with Fe³⁺ is **very high** (~38), comparable to that with Ga³⁺ (~39), indicating **strong competition**.
Ref: Zhernosekov et al., J Nucl Med 2007; **48(10):1741–1748**. Read full paper

This incident highlights how even minor metallic contamination — from something as routine as a needle — can critically impact the **efficacy, safety, and reproducibility** of radiopharmaceutical preparation.
